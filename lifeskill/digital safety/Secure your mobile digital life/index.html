    Secure your mobile digital life¶

    In the information age, our mobile phone is pretty much the most important thing in our life. It store precious memories, personal data, and financial information. What if it was suddenly gone forever in your life? This was me when I had my phones snatched in public transit.

    This page is about documentating what I have (and should have) done to prevent data loss and improve phone security, and how I react after my phone was stolen

    Security - Prevention is better than Cure¶

    The following measures can be taken to further enhance your phone security

    use a strong phone password¶

    make sure your phone has a password encrypted. Typically, a strong password will be a passphase based on multiple words with symbols in between, such as "Tofu+Turtle+Machine+2". This makes the password difficult to break but easy to remember. (You should use a password manager regardless)

    enable biometrics¶

    biometrics allows you to access applications and your phone easily without inputting any credentials. This prevents thieves to see your input before the mugging. It also makes life convenient. You can further input

    Setup a SIM Pin¶

    Also referred as Sim lock in Android Phone, this allows your SIM to be password-protected before the thief can transfer your physical sim onto another phone. This prevents your 2FA and SMS messages from being intercepted

    Setup Remote Lock¶

    This should already be enabled on Android phones by default, but it allows your phone to be locked remotely

    Setup Theft Detection Lock¶

    Introduced in Android 15, this allows your device screen to be locked if theft is detected. This is not enabled by default so make sure you have enabled it.

    Setup Offline Device Lock¶

    This will help locking your screen shortly if your device goes offline

    2FA everywhere¶

    if 2FA is available for a given application, make sure you have set it up! Having 2FA is still significantly safer than 1FA, even the authenticator itself is stored on your phone. It reduces the attack surface since the thief need to know the password still even if they own the physical device (and vice versa for online phishing)

    2FA Authenticator with passphase/biometrics¶

    It's possible to configure 2FA Authenticator to further require biometrics to login, giving you an additional layer of protection

    2FA Authenticator Sync/Backup¶

    Make sure you have backup codes for your 2FA! Authenticator such as Google Authenticator also has remote backup so at least you can restore the code once you login with your Google account, instead of getting yourself locked behind. Services like Authy also provide Cloud backup.

    Use a Password Manager¶

    Use a password manager such as Bitwarden to store your randomly generated password. Admittedly, this is quite scary since you have password stored on your phone, but it's not accessible even by thefts unless they know your master password AND your phone password access at the same time. With password manager however, you can generate strong passwords which significantly reduce the risk of online attacks

    Password Manager Sync/Backup¶

    Just like the Authenticator, you want to make sure you have a copy. Bitwarden does sync it for you already and has a function to export an encrypted vault.

    Further Pepper your password¶

    For more important passwords like bank details, rather than storing it directly in vault, you can store an incomplete password and the remaining bit somewhere else. This is what we called peppering. Some may consider redundant and increase risk of losing your password but it does give you a peace of mind if your bitwarden vault is compromised.

    secure folder¶

    Most OS, be it Android One UI or IOS, would provide a way to create a secure folder. You can put your important banking applications and Bitwarden Vault inside there.

    Be Cautious Outside¶

    Well, just don't be like me and carry a fancy phone around. Be cautious when using your phone in public space, particularly on the street and arrival at each train station. Additionally, simply avoid having your banking applications on your phone (but rather on a tablet) will save you a lot of headache.

    React - Control, Restrict, Eliminate¶

    When Shit hits the fans, these are the procedures you should take:

    Call the Police¶

    If you lost your phone on public transit in London for example, you can call British Transport Police and describe all the details. You will get a crime reference number afterwards. they probably won't provide too much help realistically (only 2% of reported crimes has a positive outcome) but who knows.

    Call your SIM Provider¶

    immediately contact your network provider to deactivate any existing SIM and replace it with a new one to prevent 2FA and SMS receival, You should still be able to get your numbers pick

    Call your Bank¶

    call your bank to let them know that your phone was stolen. They can typically arrange something for you such as freezing your card, logout on all active devices and replace your payment card (even your card is not stolen). Just because your bank account has no money, doesn't mean they cannot take a loan on behalf of your name!

    Set a withdrawl limit¶

    To reduce your financial loss, some banks should allow you to setup a maximum allowance of money you can pull in a day.

    Immediately Login to your online services¶

    Once you have your phone numbers back, you should immediately login with a different device. Some might not even need your phone number (just Authenticator or email). With Login, you can potentially kick out the active session on the stolen device

    Signout Everywhere¶

    Immediately login is actually not enough. Some services further provide option to sign out devices everywhere. Google, Outlook, Bitwarden etc

    Find your Phone¶

    Google provides find your phone functionality by default. It's more than just letting you to track your stolen device being sent to Shenzhen. You can secure or erase your devices through the find your phone hub. Additionally, you can also find your IMEI number of lost phone in here.

    Block calls except from contact¶

    If you haven't done that already, make sure you do. Enable "Do Not Disturb" mode in android and only allow calls from known contact. The thief might try to pretend themselves as bank and scam you to provide additional details. Remember, it's most likely safe when you call someone yourself, the opposite is not true!

    Be cautious with SMS¶

    You can't really block SMS like phone call does because many services used SMS for 2FA. You need to be extra cautious if scammer try to contact you back with SMS messages.
